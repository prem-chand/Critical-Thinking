# Plan 3: Entry Diagnostic & Belief Audit

**Goal:** A student can assess where they stand before starting (Month 00 diagnostic) and log their initial epistemological beliefs for year-long tracking (Belief Audit) — the two assessment artifacts that bookend the 12-month journey.

**Purpose:** The entry diagnostic prevents wrong-starts (a student who already understands Bayes should not sit through Month 03's Foundation content). The Belief Audit creates the before/after comparison that makes Month 12 meaningful. Both use Meta Bind widgets for interactivity.

**Output:** Month 00 Entry Diagnostic (20-question self-scored quiz) + Belief Audit Templater template (5-7 statements with quarterly revisit prompts).

    ```
    # Month 00 Entry Diagnostic

    ## Before You Start

    This 20-question diagnostic helps you determine where to start in the curriculum.
    There is no pass/fail — the goal is to **self-place** so you get the most out of the program.

    **Instructions:**
    1. Answer all 20 questions (4 sections, 5 questions each). Allow ~30 minutes.
    2. For each answer, also rate your confidence (0-100) using the slider.
    3. When finished, expand the "Reveal Answers" callout at the end of each section.
    4. Tally your score per section using the checklist below.
    5. Read the Starting-Month Recommendation based on your section scores.

    > [!info] Why Confidence Matters
    > Your confidence calibration (did you know when you were right?) is more important
    > than your raw score. The curriculum's calibration thread teaches you to align
    > confidence with accuracy. This diagnostic captures your starting calibration.
    ```

    **Section A: Argument Analysis** (Logic & Reasoning — 5 questions)

    Adapted from Watson-Glaser "Evaluating Arguments" format. Each question presents a research scenario and asks the student to select the best analysis of the argument.

    Question format per RESEARCH.md:
    ```markdown
    ### A1. Evaluating a Claim

    **Scenario:** A researcher claims: "Since 80% of patients who received the new treatment
    improved, while only 40% in the control group improved, the new treatment is clearly effective."

    **What is the main weakness in this reasoning?**

    `INPUT[select(
    option(), 
    option(No control for placebo effect), 
    option(Sample size is too small), 
    option(Correlation does not equal causation), 
    option(Improvement was not statistically significant)
    ):q_a1]`

    **Confidence (0-100):** `INPUT[number:q_a1_confidence]`
    ```

    Create 5 A questions covering:
    - A1: Identifying assumption vs conclusion (Watson-Glaser "Recognizing Assumptions")
    - A2: Weakening/strengthening an argument
    - A3: Identifying a premise-conclusion structure
    - A4: Distinguishing deductive vs inductive reasoning
    - A5: Spotting a circular argument

    **Section B: Probability Estimation** (Probability & Statistics — 5 questions)

    Adapted from Halpern "Likelihood & Uncertainty" format. Each question presents a scenario requiring probabilistic reasoning.

    Question format:
    ```markdown
    ### B1. Base Rate Neglect

    **Scenario:** A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 95% accurate
    (95% true positive rate, 95% true negative rate). You test positive.

    **What is the approximate probability you actually have the disease?**

    `INPUT[select(
    option(),
    option(95%),
    option(About 50%),
    option(About 16%),
    option(About 1%)
    ):q_b1]`

    **Confidence (0-100):** `INPUT[number:q_b1_confidence]`
    ```

    Create 5 B questions covering:
    - B1: Base rate neglect (Bayes)
    - B2: Gambler's fallacy / independence
    - B3: Conditional probability interpretation
    - B4: Expected value estimation
    - B5: Conjunction fallacy (Linda problem variant)

    **Section C: Evidence Evaluation** (Scientific Reasoning — 5 questions)

    Adapted from Watson-Glaser "Interpreting" + Halpern "Hypothesis Testing". Each question presents a research finding and asks the student to identify reasoning flaws about evidence.

    Question format:
    ```markdown
    ### C1. Confound Identification

    **Scenario:** A study found that students who attended more office hours had
    higher GPAs. The researchers conclude that office hours attendance causes
    better grades.

    **Which confound most undermines this conclusion?**

    `INPUT[select(
    option(),
    option(Students with higher motivation attend more office hours),
    option(Office hours are held in the library),
    option(Some professors grade more generously),
    option(The sample was self-selected)
    ):q_c1]`

    **Confidence (0-100):** `INPUT[number:q_c1_confidence]`
    ```

    Create 5 C questions covering:
    - C1: Confound identification
    - C2: P-value misinterpretation ("p < .05 means null is false")
    - C3: Sample size / statistical power intuition
    - C4: Replication vs original finding interpretation
    - C5: Correlation vs causation

    **Section D: Bias Recognition** (Metacognition & Calibration — 5 questions)

    Adapted from Halpern "Argument Analysis" + Facione "Self-Regulation". Each question presents a scenario and asks the student to identify the cognitive bias at play.

    Question format:
    ```markdown
    ### D1. Identifying Confirmation Bias

    **Scenario:** A researcher strongly believes that a new teaching method works.
    When reviewing the literature, they focus on papers supporting the method and
    dismiss contradictory studies as "poorly designed."

    **Which cognitive bias is primarily at work?**

    `INPUT[select(
    option(),
    option(Availability heuristic),
    option(Confirmation bias),
    option(Dunning-Kruger effect),
    option(Hindsight bias)
    ):q_d1]`

    **Confidence (0-100):** `INPUT[number:q_d1_confidence]`
    ```

    Create 5 D questions covering:
    - D1: Confirmation bias
    - D2: Dunning-Kruger effect (overconfidence in own ability)
    - D3: Publication bias / file drawer effect
    - D4: Anchoring on initial estimate
    - D5: Motivated reasoning (identity-protective cognition)

    **Answer Key Section** (at the bottom of each section, using Obsidian callouts):

    ```markdown
    > [!tip]- Reveal Answers — Section A
    > 
    > | Question | Correct Answer | Explanation |
    > |----------|---------------|-------------|
    > | A1 | [option text] | [brief explanation] |
    > | A2 | [option text] | [brief explanation] |
    > | A3 | [option text] | [brief explanation] |
    > | A4 | [option text] | [brief explanation] |
    > | A5 | [option text] | [brief explanation] |

    > **Score:** ___ / 5
    > 
    > **Calibration note:** Compare your confidence ratings to your accuracy.
    > If you were highly confident on wrong answers, you're overconfident in

    ```


    **Starting-Month Recommendation** (per RESEARCH.md scoring logic, adapted from CONTEXT.md D-06):

    ```markdown
    ### Recommendation Logic

    Compute your score out of 20 total (5 per section × 4 sections). Thresholds:

    | If... | Recommendation |
    |-------|---------------|
    | Any section < 4 | **Start at Month 01** — you have at least one thread needing Foundation depth |
    | All sections ≥ 4, any < 8 | **Start at Month 01** — full curriculum recommended, but you can use enrichment skips on strong threads |
    | All sections ≥ 8 | **Consider Month 04 entry** — you have solid Foundation knowledge across all 4 threads |

    > [!warning] This is a self-placement tool
    > It is NOT a validated assessment. If you're uncertain, start at Month 01.
    > Repeating Foundation material you already know takes less time than filling gaps later.

    **Your Starting Month:** ___________
    ```
